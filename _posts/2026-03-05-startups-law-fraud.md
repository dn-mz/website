Could be my algorithm but I keep seeing start-up fraud in the news. Today it's [CasStle](https://www.reuters.com/legal/government/founder-clothing-tech-startup-caastle-pleads-guilty-300-million-fraud-case-2026-03-04/){: .custom-link}; interestingly, by a senior executive in the fashion industry. A few weeks ago, [Kalder](https://www.telegraph.co.uk/business/2026/02/03/tech-30-under-30-star-faces-52-years-in-prison-for-fraud/){: .custom-link}, [ComplYant](https://www.onlycfo.io/p/sec-charges-vc-backed-company-with){: .custom-link}. The bigger names, [Frank](https://www.bbc.co.uk/news/articles/c4gwj15djdxo){: .custom-link}, [Theranos](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ndca/pr/elizabeth-holmes-sentenced-more-11-years-defrauding-theranos-investors-hundreds){: .custom-link}, and [FTX](https://www.justice.gov/archives/opa/pr/samuel-bankman-fried-sentenced-25-years-his-orchestration-multiple-fraudulent-schemes){: .custom-link}. And of course, [WeWork](https://www.profit-led-growth.com/content-hub/wework-the-47b-fraud){: .custom-link}, not fraud apparently, just "fake it till you make it" storytelling at scale.

I'm disheartened, unsurprised, yet disheartened to be unsurprised.

My co-founder and I did our rounds in US, Europe and Asia. We built and deployed robots, surpassed benchmarks, and wanted to continue building robots for the real world. Raised $8M, and ultimately turned it down. We burnt out. 

What we saw was hype-chasing, aura farming, fomo and a world where companies are built on cards. VC incentives misaligned, confused, and reward the wrong behaviour (there are of course exceptions and I've met some great VCs). It's not the game we wanted to play; we wanted to build.

Yet, with the rules of the game, it makes sense that we're seeing fraud. When capital is abundant, fomo drives decisions, and there is a pressure to perform (or appear to perform), gray area behaviour starts. Optimising for metrics. Overselling "pilots" and "verbal commitments". Gaming the growth and TAM narrative. Then, misrepresentations. Exaggerated numbers to maintain the story. Ever so often, those lines are crossed to fraud. 

I read a newly published [paper on venture fraud](https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w34868/w34868.pdf){: .custom-link}. One finding was striking: *the  VC market does not discipline fraudulent founders ex post*. Fraudulent founders go on to raise again, and their rates of founding VC-backed companies are not dissimilar to anyone else. Losses, even from fraud? Written off, part of the VC model. The paper also finds that fraud rates are increasing and that hot market conditions predict more subsequent fraud - which all make sense and part of the VC misalignment.

There's something worth naming here. We celebrate the hustle and "fake it till you make it", but don't focus on those who moved carefully, with principle, and kept their integrity. 

Perhaps honesty is boring and doesn't make for good headlines. I saw how that compounds in team trust, strong customer relationships, and solid foundations for a healthy, profitable and growing company at JustShip. I saw that with Servo too. Unfortunately, that doesn't optimise for the VC game.

There's a word for "fake it till you make it", when one doesn't make it. We, and especially VCs, don't like saying it out loud.
